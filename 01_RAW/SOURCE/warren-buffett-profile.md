# Source — Warren Buffett Profile

Warren Edward Buffett (born August 30, 1930) is an American businessman, investor, and philanthropist. He is currently the co-founder, chairman, and CEO of Berkshire Hathaway. As a result of his immense investment success, Buffett is one of the best-known investors in the world and has consistently been ranked among the wealthiest individuals globally.

## Key Takeaways

- **Value Investing and Business Quality**: Buying wonderful businesses at fair prices rather than fair businesses at wonderful prices.
- **Rational Capital Allocation**: Regulating the reinvestment of company earnings into high-return opportunities or returning it to shareholders.
- **The Circle of Competence**: Avoiding areas of ignorance and keeping investment decisions strictly within what is thoroughly understood.
- **Long-term Compounding**: The exponential multiplication of capital over long holding periods using the power of float and retained earnings.
- **Emotional Temperament**: Staying rational when others are greedy or fearful.

## Profile Details

### Early Life & Ventures
Warren Buffett was born in Omaha, Nebraska. The son of Howard Buffett, a local congressman and stockbroker, Warren showed an early interest in business. At age 11, he bought his first stock (Cities Service Preferred). In childhood, he engaged in multiple entrepreneurial ventures, including selling chewing gum door-to-door, selling Coca-Cola bottles, retrieving and selling golf balls, and running a massive paper route with multiple delivery networks.

### Education & Graham
Buffett studied at the University of Nebraska and went on to Columbia Business School, where he studied under Benjamin Graham, the father of value investing. Graham taught Buffett to view stocks as fractional ownership of businesses and introduced the concepts of "Intrinsic Value" and the "Margin of Safety." After graduation, Buffett worked briefly for Graham's firm before returning to Omaha to start the Buffett Partnership.

### Berkshire Hathaway & Munger
In 1962, Buffett began buying shares in Berkshire Hathaway, a declining textile mill. He eventually took control of the company, a move he later called his "most monumental mistake" because it tied up substantial capital in a dying industry. However, he used Berkshire's cash flows to acquire insurance companies (beginning with National Indemnity), converting Berkshire into a massive holding company.
In 1959, Buffett met Charlie Munger. Munger eventually became the Vice Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway. Munger influenced Buffett to move away from Graham's strict "cigar butt" style of investing (buying low-quality companies at extremely cheap prices) toward buying high-quality businesses with strong competitive advantages (pricing power, brand equity, economic moats) at fair prices.

### Core Investment Principles
- **Intrinsic Value**: The discounted value of cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life.
- **Margin of Safety**: The difference between market price and intrinsic value that protects against errors in calculation or adverse business developments.
- **Economic Moat**: A business's sustainable competitive advantage that allows it to maintain high returns on capital against competition.
- **Circle of Competence**: The boundary of a person's knowledge and expertise. Buffett advocates staying inside it to avoid high-risk errors.
- **Capital Allocation**: The CEO's primary job is to allocate cash flows efficiently among organic expansion, acquisitions, stock buybacks, or dividends.

### Habits & Temperament
- **Reading**: Buffett reads 500 pages a day, noting that "knowledge builds up, like compound interest."
- **Patience**: Buffett compares investing to a baseball game with "no called strikes"—investors can wait for the perfect pitch.
- **Frugal Lifestyle**: Despite his billions, Buffett lives in the same house in Omaha he bought in 1958 and drives a modest car.
