# Cash Register Automation

## Core idea
In the mid-1800s, supply store owner John Henry Patterson in Dayton, Ohio, struggled to make a profit despite running a busy coal-miner supply store with little competition. He soon realized that his employees were stealing his cash. In that era, cash drawers were kept open and receipts could easily be discarded or altered without tracking.

Patterson came across an advertisement for a new invention by James Ritty called **Ritty's Incorruptible Cashier**—the first cash register. The machine automatically locked cash and transaction receipts inside after every sale. Patterson purchased two of these cash registers for fifty dollars each, and employee theft vanished overnight. Within six months, the store turned a profit of $5,000 (equivalent to over $100,000 today). Impressed by this result, Patterson purchased the rights to Ritty's invention and founded the National Cash Register Company.

## Why it matters / connection
This example illustrates how technology and physical automation can make good habits (ethical transaction tracking) inevitable and bad habits (employee theft) impossible. Rather than trying to change human nature or supervise employees constant, the physical cash register restructured the environment to enforce the desired behavior automatically. It shows that the best way to break a bad habit is to make it practical and physical friction-wise impossible.
