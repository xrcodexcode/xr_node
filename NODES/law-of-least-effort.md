# Law of Least Effort

## Core idea
The Law of Least Effort states that when deciding between two similar options, individuals will naturally gravitate toward the option that requires the least amount of work. This is a fundamental law of human behavior rooted in biology and physics (analogous to the Principle of Least Action). Because energy is precious, the human brain is evolutionary wired to conserve it whenever possible.

Every habit is essentially an obstacle to obtaining what you actually want (e.g., dieting is an obstacle to getting fit, journaling is an obstacle to thinking clearly). Cravings are not for the habit itself, but for the outcome it delivers. Thus, the greater the friction or obstacle between the current state and the desired state, the less likely the behavior is to occur.

An example of this principle on a global scale is the spread of agriculture: as documented by Jared Diamond, agriculture spread two to three times faster along the east-west axis of Europe and Asia than it did along the north-south axis of the Americas and Africa because locations along the same latitude share similar climates and seasons, minimizing the agricultural friction for farmers.

## Why it matters / connection
This concept underpins the 3rd Law of Behavior Change: **Make It Easy**. Rather than attempting to pump up motivation to force oneself through high-friction habits (which is like trying to force water through a bent garden hose), one should design environments to remove the friction points (removing the bend in the hose). By making good habits convenient and low friction, they become the path of least resistance.
