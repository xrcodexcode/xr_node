# Identity-based Habits

## Core idea
Behavior change occurs at three distinct levels, which can be visualized as the layers of an onion:
1. **Outcomes** (outer layer): What you get (e.g., losing weight, winning a championship, publishing a book).
2. **Processes** (middle layer): What you do (e.g., implementing a new gym routine, decluttering a desk, developing a meditation practice).
3. **Identity** (innermost layer): What you believe (e.g., your worldview, self-image, beliefs, and judgments about yourself and others).

Most people build **outcome-based habits**, starting from what they want to achieve and working inward. This fails because they attempt to change their actions without updating the underlying beliefs driving them. The alternative is to build **identity-based habits**, starting with who you wish to become.

For example, when offered a cigarette:
- An outcome-based attempt: "No thanks. I'm trying to quit." (Still identifies as a smoker trying to be something else).
- An identity-based approach: "No thanks. I'm not a smoker." (Signals a shift in identity; smoking is no longer part of who they are).

## Why it matters / connection
Behavior that is incongruent with the self will not last. A person can motivate themselves to go to the gym or eat healthily a few times, but if their self-image remains that of someone who prioritizes comfort over physical training, they will eventually revert to old routines. True behavior change is identity change. The ultimate goal is not to read a book, run a marathon, or learn an instrument, but rather to *become* a reader, a runner, or a musician.
