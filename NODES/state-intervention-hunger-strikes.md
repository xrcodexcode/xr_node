# State Intervention in Hunger Strikes

## Summary
In India, the state has a established legal and operational framework for intervening in hunger strikes, particularly those declared "unto death". Under the justification of preventing suicide (a state responsibility to protect life), authorities have the legal right to arrest protestors, hospitalize them without explicit consent, and administer medical drips to maintain their health.

## Key Idea
Hunger strikes create a direct confrontation of consent between the citizen's right to fast as a peaceful protest and the state's mandate to prevent suicide, with the legal system prioritizing the state's right to force medical intervention.

## Details
* **The Suicide Loophole:** Because a hunger strike "unto death" threatens self-harm, the state uses laws against suicide to execute arrests. This allows the state to bypass the protestor's lack of consent.
* **Standard Operating Procedure (SOP):** Once arrested, the protestor is transferred to a hospital ward where intravenous (IV) fluids are administered. This effectively neutralizes the immediate physical threat of the hunger strike while keeping the protestor in custody.
* **Irom Sharmila Case Study:** The longest and most famous example is Irom Sharmila (the "Iron Lady of Manipur"), who protested the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act (AFSPA) from 2000 to 2016. The state repeatedly arrested her on charges of attempted suicide, held her in a hospital under forced nasal feeding for the legal maximum of one year, released her, and immediately re-arrested her when she resumed fasting. This cycle was repeated consistently for 16 years.
