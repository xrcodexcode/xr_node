# Anne Thorndike Cafeteria Study

## Core idea
In 2001, Dr. Anne Thorndike, a primary care physician at Massachusetts General Hospital in Boston, conducted a six-month study to improve the eating and drinking habits of thousands of hospital staff and visitors. She aimed to change their behavior without talking to them, attempting to motivate them, or relying on their willpower.

Thorndike altered the **"choice architecture"** of the hospital cafeteria:
- **Before**: The refrigerators located next to the cash registers were filled only with soda.
- **After**: Water was added as an option inside each of those refrigerators, and baskets of bottled water were placed near food stations throughout the room. Soda remained in the primary refrigerators, but water was now highly visible and accessible at all drink locations.

The results after three months were significant:
- Soda sales dropped by **11.4%**.
- Bottled water sales increased by **25.8%**.

Similar adjustments were made to the cafeteria's food selection and arrangement, yielding similar shifts toward healthier options.

## Why it matters / connection
This study demonstrates that human behavior is heavily context-dependent and shaped by the environment. People often choose products not out of conscious desire, but because of their proximity and visual salience. It challenges the assumption that behavior change requires willpower, showing instead that designing an environment with obvious cues naturally leads to better choices. It connects directly to environment design as a primary tool for habit construction.
