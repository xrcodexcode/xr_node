# Solomon Asch Conformity Experiment

## Core idea
In the 1950s, psychologist Solomon Asch conducted a series of social conformity experiments. A subject entered a room with several actors who were posing as participants. The group was shown a card with a line on it, followed by a second card with three lines (A, B, and C) of different lengths. The task was simple: select which comparison line matched the target line.

During initial rounds, everyone agreed on the obvious correct answer. However, in subsequent trials, the actors were instructed to deliver a scripted, intentionally incorrect answer. As the number of actors in the room increased (up to eight), the pressure on the subject grew. By the end of the experiment, nearly 75% of the subjects conformed to the group's incorrect answer at least once, doubting their own senses.

## Why it matters / connection
This study demonstrates the immense internal pressure individuals face to comply with social norms. The reward of being accepted by the group is often valued more highly than the reward of being correct. This biological drive to get along with the tribe explains why changing habits is difficult when it requires challenging the group, and why habits are far easier to maintain when they align with the behaviors of the surrounding culture.
