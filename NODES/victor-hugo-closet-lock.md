# Victor Hugo Closet Lock

## Core idea
In the summer of 1830, French author Victor Hugo faced an impossible deadline. He had promised his publisher a new book twelve months prior but had spent the year socializing, entertaining guests, and procrastinating. The publisher set a strict, final deadline: the book had to be finished by February 1831.

To defeat his procrastination, Hugo devised a plan. He gathered all of his clothes and instructed his assistant to lock them away in a large chest, leaving him with nothing to wear except a large knitted shawl. Deprived of suitable clothing to go outdoors, Hugo was forced to remain in his study and write furiously throughout the fall and winter. As a result, *The Hunchback of Notre Dame* was completed and published two weeks early on January 14, 1831.

## Why it matters / connection
This is a classic historical example of a commitment device. Rather than relying on willpower or hoping his motivation would increase, Hugo modified his environment to make his bad habit (going outside/socializing) physically impossible, and his good habit (writing) the path of least resistance. It highlights how structuring friction in advance can lock in desired future behaviors.
