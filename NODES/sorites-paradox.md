# Sorites Paradox

## Core idea
The Sorites Paradox (also known as the Paradox of the Heap) is an ancient Greek parable that illustrates the compounding effect of repeating small actions:
* **The formulation**: Can a single coin make a person rich? If you give someone ten coins, they are not rich. If you add one more coin, they are still not rich. Yet, if you continue to add one coin at a time, you must eventually admit that the person is rich. Therefore, a series of seemingly insignificant additions eventually triggers a transformation.

In behavior change, atomic habits operate under the same paradox:
* **The Accumulation of Change**: A single tiny change (like reading one page or meditating for one minute) is unlikely to notice-ably transform your life. In the beginning, these small positive steps are washed away by the weight of the existing, larger system.
* **The Tipping Point**: As you layer small 1 percent improvements on top of one another, you slowly add grains of sand to the scale. Eventually, the cumulative weight reaches a tipping point, and the system begins working *for* you rather than against you, making good habits feel effortless.

Success is not a single finish line to cross, but a continuous system to improve and refine.

## Why it matters / connection
The Sorites Paradox provides the philosophical foundation for the entire book. It explains why we must trust the process of daily compounding gains even when we are in the [[plateau-of-latent-potential]] and see no immediate results. It connects to [[one-percent-better-every-day]] and highlights the superiority of [[systems-vs-goals]].
