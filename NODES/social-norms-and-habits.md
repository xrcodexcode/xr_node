# Social Norms and Habits

## Core idea
Humans are herd animals driven by a deep evolutionary desire to belong, fit in, and win the approval of their peers. Historically, being separated or cast out from the tribe was a death sentence, meaning that collaboration and bonding were essential for survival. In the modern world, this manifests as an imitation of social norms—the invisible rules that guide behavior daily. We do not choose our earliest habits; rather, we inherit them by imitating three primary social groups:
1. **The Close**: We pick up habits from family, friends, and coworkers through physical and social proximity. As a general rule, the closer we are to someone, the more likely we are to copy their habits (e.g., studies show a person's risk of becoming obese increases by 57% if they have a friend who becomes obese).
2. **The Many**: We look to the crowd to guide our behavior when we are unsure. Solomon Asch's conformity experiments showed that nearly 75% of subjects conformed to obviously incorrect answers when surrounded by actors giving the same wrong response.
3. **The Powerful**: We mimic high-status individuals who possess power, prestige, and status because we desire the same respect, approval, and admiration they receive.

## Why it matters / connection
One of the most effective strategies to build better habits is to join a culture where the desired behavior is already the normal behavior, especially if you already share a common bond with the group (e.g., Nerd Fitness clients bonding over Star Wars while getting in shape). Conversely, when changing a habit requires challenging the norms of the tribe, behavior change becomes highly unattractive because we would rather be wrong with the crowd than be right by ourselves.
