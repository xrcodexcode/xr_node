# Bryan Harris Habit Contract

## Core idea
Bryan Harris, an entrepreneur from Nashville, Tennessee, successfully used a formal habit contract to lose weight and maintain fitness after the birth of his son. Harris drafted a physical document signed by himself, his wife, and his personal trainer Joey, outlining his long-term goal of reaching 200 pounds at 10% body fat.

The contract detailed:
* **The Rules**: Daily logging of all food consumed and daily weigh-ins.
* **Phase 1 Consequences**: If he missed logging his food or weighing in, he had to pay his trainer $200 and dress up for work and church for the rest of the quarter (defined as no jeans, t-shirts, hoodies, or shorts).
* **Escalating Consequences**: In subsequent phases, the stakes were raised:
  * Paying his trainer $100 for missing macro targets.
  * Paying his wife $500 for failing to weigh himself.
  * Wearing an Alabama hat (the bitter rival of his beloved Auburn college team) and dressing up for work every day if he forgot to run sprints.

The presence of his wife and trainer as active accountability partners, coupled with the signed document, made the commitment serious and prevented Harris from slacking.

## Why it matters / connection
This example illustrates how to design and execute a practical [[habit-contracts]]. It shows that formalizing a contract with signatures and escalating, highly personal penalties (both financial and social) creates the necessary immediate friction to override the temptation of short-term bad habits, linking directly to the inversion of the 4th Law (Make It Unsatisfying).
