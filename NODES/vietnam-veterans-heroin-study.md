# Vietnam Veterans Heroin Study

## Core idea
In 1971, during the Vietnam War, U.S. Congressmen Robert Steele and Morgan Murphy discovered that over 15% of American soldiers stationed in Vietnam were addicted to heroin. A follow-up study revealed that 35% of service members had tried heroin and up to 20% were actively addicted. In response, President Richard Nixon created the Special Action Office of Drug Abuse Prevention to track and rehabilitate these service members when they returned.

Researcher Lee Robins was put in charge of tracking these veterans. The study upended the medical understanding of addiction at the time:
- Only **5%** of addicted veterans became readdicted within one year of returning home.
- Only **12%** relapsed within three years.
- Nearly **9 out of 10** soldiers who were addicted to heroin in Vietnam eliminated their addiction almost overnight.

This contrasted sharply with civilian drug rehabilitation, where **90%** of heroin users relapse after returning home from a clinic.

### The Environmental Explanation
In Vietnam, soldiers were constantly exposed to environmental cues that prompted heroin use:
- Easy access and low cost of the drug.
- The severe, everyday stress of war.
- A peer group consisting of other heroin users.
- Isolation from their families and home environment.

Upon returning to the United States, soldiers entered a completely different environment, devoid of all the triggers that had prompted drug use in Vietnam. When the context changed, the habit dissolved.

## Why it matters / connection
This study challenges the cultural belief that bad habits and addictions are signs of moral weakness or a lack of personal self-control. It demonstrates that habits are context-dependent, and that rather than relying solely on willpower, the most effective way to eliminate a bad habit is to remove the environmental cues that trigger it.
