# Habit Loop

## Core idea
The **Habit Loop** is a four-step feedback loop that governs how habits are formed, maintained, and repeated. It synthesizes cognitive and behavioral sciences into a model that accounts for both external triggers and internal emotional states. The loop consists of:

```
          [1. Cue] (Problem Phase)
             |
             v
         [2. Craving] (Problem Phase)
             |
             v
        [3. Response] (Solution Phase)
             |
             v
         [4. Reward] (Solution Phase)
```

### The Four Steps
1. **Cue**: An environmental trigger that prompts the brain to initiate a behavior. It is a piece of sensory information that predicts a reward (e.g., waking up, smelling a bakery, seeing a red light).
2. **Craving**: The motivational force behind every habit. You do not crave the habit itself, but rather the change in state it delivers. For example, you do not crave smoking a cigarette; you crave the feeling of relief it provides. Cravings are context-dependent and determined by thoughts and emotions.
3. **Response**: The actual habit you perform, which can take the form of a physical action or a mental routine. Whether a response occurs depends on your level of motivation and the amount of friction (physical or mental effort) involved.
4. **Reward**: The final destination of the loop. Rewards serve two purposes: they satisfy the craving (providing immediate relief or pleasure) and they teach our brain which actions are worth remembering and repeating in the future.

### The Two Phases
- **Problem Phase**: Includes the **Cue** and the **Craving**. You notice a stimulus and desire to change your internal state.
- **Solution Phase**: Includes the **Response** and the **Reward**. You take action and obtain the desired state, reinforcing the association with the cue.

### Historical Context
James Clear’s model expands on previous behavioral science frameworks:
- **B.F. Skinner** (1930s operant conditioning): "Stimulus, Response, Reward." Skinner's model focused purely on external behavior and rewards.
- **Charles Duhigg** (*The Power of Habit*): "Cue, Routine, Reward." 
Clear’s addition of **Craving** integrates cognitive science, explaining how our thoughts, feelings, and beliefs translate external cues into behavioral responses.

## Why it matters / connection
The Habit Loop is the operational engine of behavior change. If a behavior fails at any of the four stages, it will not become a habit:
- Without a **Cue**, a habit will never start.
- Without a **Craving**, there is no motivation to act.
- Without an easy **Response**, the behavior is too difficult to perform.
- Without a **Reward**, there is no reason to repeat the action.

The loop directly informs the **Four Laws of Behavior Change** (Make it Obvious, Make it Attractive, Make it Easy, Make it Satisfying).
