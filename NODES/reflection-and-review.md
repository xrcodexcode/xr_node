# Reflection and Review

## Core idea
Reflection and review is the antidote to the lack of self-awareness and performance decline that occurs when habits become automatic. It enables long-term course corrections by making you conscious of mistakes and clarifying paths for improvement.

Examples of reflection and review systems include:
* **Career Best Effort (CBE) Program**: Used by Pat Riley with the Los Angeles Lakers. The system tracked baseline statistics, demanded a 1% seasonal improvement, credited "unsung hero" deeds (like diving for loose balls), and compared players against historical and league-wide metrics. It led the team to back-to-back NBA championships in 1987 and 1988.
* **Top Athlete Tracking**: Marathoner Eliud Kipchoge takes notes after every practice to review training; swimmer Katie Ledecky logs wellness, nutrition, sleep quality, and competitor times.
* **Chris Rock's Comedy Testing**: Testing hundreds of jokes at small nightclubs with a notepad to record which lines get laughs and refine the material.

James Clear's personal reflection systems include:
1. **The Annual Review** (Each December): Tallying habits (articles published, workouts, travel) and answering:
   1. *What went well this year?*
   2. *What didn't go so well this year?*
   3. *What did I learn?*
2. **The Integrity Report** (Each Summer): Aligning habits with identity and values, answering:
   1. *What are the core values that drive my life and work?*
   2. *How am I living and working with integrity right now?*
   3. *How can I set a higher standard in the future?*

Additionally, reflection helps you **keep your identity small** (as investor Paul Graham advises). Clinging too tightly to a single belief or role (e.g., "I'm the CEO" or "I'm an athlete") makes you brittle and vulnerable to an identity crisis when circumstances change. Redefine yourself around flexible traits (e.g., "I am the type of person who is disciplined and loves a challenge").

## Why it matters / connection
This method ensures that habit automation does not lead to stagnation or complacency. By periodically stepping back to view habits from a "conversational distance" (like a mirror), you get enough feedback to make adjustments without being overwhelmed by daily noise.
