# Goldilocks Rule

## Core idea
The Goldilocks Rule states that humans experience peak motivation when working on tasks that are right on the edge of their current abilities. Not too hard, not too easy, just right.

The three difficulty zones:
* **Too Easy**: Playing a tennis match against a four-year-old leads to immediate boredom. If there is no challenge, your focus shifts and motivation drops.
* **Too Hard**: Playing against a professional like Roger Federer or Serena Williams leads to immediate frustration and loss of motivation. If there is no hope of success, you quit.
* **Just Manageable Difficulty**: Playing against someone who is your equal. You win some points and lose some. Focus narrows, distractions fade, and you become fully invested.

Scientific and psychological connections:
1. **Yerkes-Dodson Law**: This law describes the optimal level of arousal as the midpoint between boredom (too easy) and anxiety (too hard).
2. **Flow State**: An immersive state of being "in the zone," which researchers find is triggered when a task is roughly 4 percent beyond your current ability.
3. **Steve Martin's Comedy Career**: Steve Martin spent 18 years practicing and refining his routines, expanding them by only a minute or two each year, ensuring enough victories to stay motivated and enough mistakes to keep working.

## Why it matters / connection
While the 3rd Law of Behavior Change (Make It Easy) is essential for *starting* a habit, the Goldilocks Rule is critical for *sustaining* it. Once a habit is established, variety and progressive challenge are necessary to prevent boredom—which is the greatest threat to success, even more than failure. Staying in the Goldilocks zone keeps behavior attractive and satisfying.
