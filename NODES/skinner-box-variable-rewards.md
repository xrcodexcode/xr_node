# Skinner Box Variable Rewards

## Core idea
B.F. Skinner’s research on operant conditioning (often using a "Skinner Box" containing animals like rats or pigeons) led to the discovery of **variable rewards**. A variable reward is a payoff that is delivered at unpredictable intervals rather than on a fixed, predictable schedule (similar to a slot machine).

Key aspects of variable rewards include:
* **Dopamine Spikes**: The uncertainty and variance of the reward lead to the greatest spikes of dopamine in the brain, which enhances memory recall and accelerates habit formation.
* **Reducing Boredom**: Variable rewards do not create a craving out of nothing, but they are highly effective at amplifying existing cravings by keeping the experience novel and surprising.
* **The 50/50 Sweet Spot**: The peak of desire occurs when there is a 50/50 split between success and failure—offering enough winning to satisfy, but enough wanting to sustain interest.
* **Modern Applications**: Many habit-forming products rely on continuous visual, culinary, or social novelty to drive compulsive behavior (e.g., social media feeds, video games, junk food, and gambling).

## Why it matters / connection
This concept explains the psychological mechanism behind addictive and compulsive behaviors in our environment. It connects to the [[dopamine-driven-feedback-loop]]. While we want utility to be predictable (e.g., search engines or ride-sharing), variable rewards explain why we get hooked on feedback loops and highlight the importance of the [[goldilocks-rule]] in keeping habits engaging without slipping into harmful addiction.
