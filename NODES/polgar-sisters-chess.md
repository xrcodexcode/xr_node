# Polgar Sisters Chess

## Core idea
In 1965, Hungarian teacher Laszlo Polgar initiated a plan to raise his future children to become chess prodigies to test his belief that "genius is not born, but is educated and trained." He rejected the concept of innate talent, claiming deliberate practice and environmental design could turn any child into a genius. Together with his wife Klara, they home-schooled their three daughters (Susan, Sofia, and Judit) in an environment entirely dedicated to chess. The home was filled with chess books, pictures of famous players, and records of competitors.

The experiment was extremely successful:
- **Susan** (oldest): Began playing at age four, and was defeating adults within six months.
- **Sofia** (middle): Became a world champion at fourteen and subsequently a grandmaster.
- **Judit** (youngest): Beat her father at age five, entered the world's top one hundred players at twelve, and became the youngest grandmaster of all time at fifteen years and four months.

Despite this intense upbringing, the sisters did not find their childhood grueling. They loved playing chess and found it enjoyable because they grew up in a micro-culture where chess was prioritized, praised, and rewarded.

## Why it matters / connection
This case study highlights the profound influence of culture and environmental design on habits. Because chess was the normal, highly rewarded behavior in their household, the sisters found the grueling daily practice attractive and enjoyable rather than burdensome. It demonstrates how behavior is guided by what is valued by our social circle, linking to the concept of identity-based habits and social norms.
