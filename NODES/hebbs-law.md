# Hebb's Law

## Core idea
First described by neuropsychologist Donald Hebb in 1949, Hebb's Law states that **"neurons that fire together wire together."** This refers to the neurological phenomenon of long-term potentiation, where repeated activation of a specific neural circuit strengthens the connections between neurons, making cell-to-cell signaling faster and more efficient.

With each repetition of a behavior, the physical structure of the brain adapts to become more efficient at that activity (e.g., London taxi drivers showing an enlarged hippocampus for spatial memory, or musicians having a larger cerebellum for fine motor control). This process drives the transition from conscious, effortful behavior to nonconscious, automatic execution—a threshold referred to as the **Habit Line**.

## Why it matters / connection
Hebb's Law proves that habit formation is driven by the frequency of repetition, not the mere passage of time. It is not about whether a habit has been practiced for twenty-one or thirty days, but how many times it has been repeated. By continually repeating a behavior, the brain builds the neural pathways necessary to automate the habit, making active repetition the single most critical step in behavior change.
