# Trent Dyrsmid Paper Clips

## Core idea
In 1993, a twenty-three-year-old rookie stockbroker named Trent Dyrsmid was hired at a bank in Abbotsford, Canada. Despite being a rookie in a small suburb away from the main financial hub, Dyrsmid achieved rapid, outstanding success through a simple daily tracking ritual:
* He began each morning with two jars on his desk: one filled with 120 paper clips, and the other empty.
* After making a sales call, he would immediately move one paper clip from the full jar to the empty jar.
* He repeated this process, dialing the phone continuously, until all 120 paper clips were moved to the second jar.

Within eighteen months, Dyrsmid brought in $5 million to his firm. By age twenty-four, he was earning the equivalent of $125,000 in today's currency and shortly thereafter landed a six-figure job elsewhere.

This technique is known as the **Paper Clip Strategy**. It leverages visual measures of progress to provide clear, immediate, and satisfying feedback on repetitive tasks. It has since been adapted by others using different objects, such as hairpins for writing book pages or marbles for counting push-ups.

## Why it matters / connection
This example demonstrates the power of the 4th Law of Behavior Change (Make It Satisfying) and serves as a concrete application of [[habit-tracking]]. Repetitive, high-friction habits (like cold calling) are difficult to sustain because their rewards are delayed. By using paper clips as a visual and tactile feedback loop, Dyrsmid made the process satisfying in the moment, enabling him to remain consistent and compound his results.
