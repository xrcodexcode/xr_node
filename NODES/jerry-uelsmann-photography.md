# Jerry Uelsmann Photography Experiment

## Core idea
Jerry Uelsmann, a professor of film photography at the University of Florida, conducted an experiment with his class. He divided the students into two groups:
1. **The "Quantity" Group**: Graded solely on the volume of work they produced. A student who submitted one hundred photos received an A, ninety photos a B, eighty photos a C, and so on.
2. **The "Quality" Group**: Graded solely on the excellence of their work. They only needed to submit a single, nearly perfect photo at the end of the semester to get an A.

At the end of the term, Uelsmann discovered that all of the best photos were produced by the quantity group. While the quantity group was busy taking hundreds of photos, experimenting with lighting, composition, and darkroom techniques, they actively honed their skills through trial and error. Meanwhile, the quality group sat around theorizing about perfection and ultimately produced only a mediocre photo.

## Why it matters / connection
This experiment illustrates the critical difference between being in **motion** (planning, strategizing, learning) and taking **action** (performing behaviors that yield outcomes). It shows that behavior change and skill mastery require starting with active repetition rather than passive theorizing. To master any habit, one must focus on putting in repetitions ("getting the reps in") rather than waiting for a perfect plan.
