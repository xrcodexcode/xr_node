# Supernormal Stimulus

## Core idea
A supernormal stimulus is an exaggerated, heightened version of a natural cue that elicits a stronger behavioral response than the original, realistic cue would. 

The concept was pioneered in the 1940s by Dutch scientist Niko Tinbergen, who won a Nobel Prize for his work. In his experiments:
- **Herring Gulls**: Newly hatched chicks pecked at a red dot on their parent's beak to ask for food. Tinbergen offered them cardboard dummy beaks. The chicks pecked at them readily. When he presented a dummy beak with three large red dots, the chicks pecked at it with extreme excitement, preferring the exaggerated fake over their real mothers.
- **Greylag Geese**: Geese roll stray eggs back into their ground nests. Tinbergen discovered they would roll back any round object (billiard balls, lightbulbs). The larger the object, the greater their effort. One goose even attempted to roll and sit on a volleyball.

Humans are equally vulnerable to supernormal stimuli. Modern industries design exaggerated cues to hijack our Paleolithic brain instincts, which evolved in environments of scarcity:
1. **Junk Food**: Engineered to optimize fat, salt, and sugar. Food scientists focus on *orosensation* (mouthfeel, like the crunch of a chip) and *dynamic contrast* (combinations of crunch and creaminess, like Oreo cookies or crispy pizza with melted cheese) to find the "bliss point" that excites the brain and leads to overeating.
2. **Social Media**: Delivers highly concentrated amounts of social praise, validation, and "likes" in a few minutes, surpassing what our ancestors could earn in a lifetime.
3. **Advertising**: Uses perfect lighting, professional makeup, and digital editing to show features that are naturally attractive, driving excessive shopping.
4. **Online Pornography**: Splices together stimulating scenes at an impossible rate compared to real-world interactions.

## Why it matters / connection
Understanding supernormal stimuli is key to applying the Second Law of Behavior Change: *Make It Attractive*. Modern temptations are highly engineered versions of reality that are hard to resist because they stimulate reward centers that haven't changed for 50,000 years. To build better habits, we must understand how to bundle or design cues that make good habits attractive, and invert the law to make bad habits unattractive by removing or reframing these supernormal cues.
