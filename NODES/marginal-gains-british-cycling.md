# Marginal Gains British Cycling

## Core idea
In 2003, British Cycling hired Dave Brailsford as its performance director. At the time, professional cycling in Great Britain was in a century-long state of mediocrity: British riders had won only a single gold medal at the Olympic Games since 1908 and had never won the Tour de France in 110 years. The team's performance was so poor that a top European manufacturer refused to sell them bikes, fearing it would hurt their brand.

Brailsford committed to a philosophy he called "the aggregation of marginal gains"—improving every component of riding a bike by just 1% to get a significant cumulative increase. 

The team implemented small adjustments:
- **Equipment & Apparel**: Redesigned bike seats for comfort, rubbed alcohol on tires for better grip, had riders wear electrically heated overshorts to maintain muscle temperature, and tested fabrics in a wind tunnel, leading outdoor riders to adopt lighter, more aerodynamic indoor racing suits.
- **Biometrics & Training**: Used biofeedback sensors to monitor workout responses.
- **Unconventional Optimization**: Tested different massage gels for muscle recovery, hired a surgeon to teach riders proper handwashing to prevent colds, selected customized pillows and mattresses for optimal sleep, and painted the inside of the team truck white to spot tiny specs of dust that could degrade bike performance.

The accumulated improvements led to unprecedented success:
- At the **2008 Beijing Olympics**, British Cycling won 60% of all available gold medals.
- At the **2012 London Olympics**, they set nine Olympic records and seven world records.
- In **2012**, Bradley Wiggins became the first Briton to win the Tour de France.
- His teammate Chris Froome won the Tour de France in **2013, 2015, 2016, and 2017**.
- Between **2007 and 2017**, they captured 178 world championships, 66 Olympic/Paralympic gold medals, and five Tour de France victories.

## Why it matters / connection
This study is the ultimate real-world proof of how habits compound. It highlights the difference between goals (wanting to win a championship) and systems (daily 1% improvements). Brailsford shifted the focus from a single, massive leap to a systematic breakdown of inputs, demonstrating that compounding interest applies to physical performance and execution.
