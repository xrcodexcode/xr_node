# Pointing-and-Calling

## Core idea
Pointing-and-Calling is a safety system designed to reduce human error by prompting operators to physically point at key indicators and verbally state their status. It is famously used in the Japanese railway system. For example, a train conductor will point at a signal and say, "Signal is green," point at the speedometer and state the exact speed, and point at the timetable before departing.

This ritual makes nonconscious, automatic patterns conscious. Research shows that Pointing-and-Calling reduces errors by up to 85 percent and cuts accidents by 30 percent. A modified "point-only" version adopted by the New York City MTA subway system reduced incorrectly berthed subways by 57 percent within two years of implementation.

On a personal level, Pointing-and-Calling can be used to prevent bad habits by speaking actions and their consequences aloud. For instance, if you find yourself reaching for an unnecessary snack, saying aloud: "I'm about to eat this cookie, but I don't need it. Eating it will cause me to gain weight and hurt my health" forces the brain to consciously acknowledge the action, breaking the autopilot loop.

## Why it matters / connection
Many of our failures in performance are due to a lack of self-awareness. As habits become automatic, we stop paying attention to what we are doing. Pointing-and-Calling serves as a mechanism to interrupt autopilot behavior and re-engage the conscious mind before mistakes or bad habits occur.
