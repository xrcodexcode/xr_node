# E20 Fuel Policy in India

## Summary
The E20 Fuel Policy in India mandates a fuel blend consisting of 80% petrol and 20% ethanol. The policy was introduced to enhance energy security, reduce India's heavy dependency on imported crude oil (which accounts for ~85% of total consumption), and support domestic farmers by redirecting surplus sugarcane and grain crop production toward ethanol manufacturing.

## Key Idea
Although intended as a strategic move toward energy independence and agricultural support, the accelerated implementation of the E20 policy bypassed standard consumer automotive lifecycles, causing engine degradation in older vehicles.

## Details
* **Timeline Acceleration:** The original target for achieving a nationwide E20 blend was set for 2030. However, the government fast-tracked the timeline, achieving widespread distribution at petrol stations by 2025.
* **Compatibility Mismatch:** While all passenger vehicles manufactured after April 1, 2023, are legally mandated to be E20-compliant, the vast majority of older vehicles on Indian roads were designed only for E10 (10% ethanol blend). The average replacement cycle for a car in India is 7 to 10 years, meaning millions of E10-compatible cars are forced to run on corrosive E20 fuel.
* **Material Damage:** Ethanol is highly hygroscopic (it absorbs water from the air) and corrosive. When used in non-E20-compatible engines, it slowly degrades rubber hoses, PVC pipes, fuel pump seals, and metal fuel lines.
* **Narrative Positioning:** The policy is heavily promoted by state communication channels as a green initiative that supports "national interest," "farmer income," and "energy sovereignty," which has effectively suppressed consumer debates on engine damage and lower mileage.
