# SpaceX Falcon 1 Launch Failures

The first three Falcon 1 launches failed (2006-2008). SpaceX had money for exactly one more attempt. The fourth succeeded, saving the company.

## Explanation

**Flight 1 (March 24, 2006)**: Failed 25 seconds after liftoff due to a fuel-line fire that breached a helium line. Cause: a corroded aluminum nut on a fuel-line fitting. Lesson: even tiny components can destroy a rocket.

**Flight 2 (March 21, 2007)**: Reached space but second stage failed to reach orbit. Cause: fuel slosh caused the engine to shut down early. Lesson: fluid dynamics in microgravity must be engineered for, not assumed.

**Flight 3 (August 3, 2008)**: First stage and second stage collided during separation. Cause: the separation system failed due to residual thrust. Lesson: never assume separation timing.

**Flight 4 (September 28, 2008)**: Success — Falcon 1 became the first privately developed liquid-fueled rocket to reach orbit.

## Why it matters

Musk had bet his remaining capital on Flight 4. When asked at NASA how much it cost to develop Falcon 1, he said: "Everything I had." Each failure taught specific engineering lessons that informed Falcon 9's design. The failures were not signs of incompetence; they were the necessary cost of learning in a domain where simulation cannot replicate reality.
