# Roger Fisher Nuclear Button

## Core idea
Roger Fisher, a Harvard Law professor specializing in conflict management and negotiation, proposed a thought experiment in 1981 to prevent nuclear war. He noticed that any sitting President has access to launch codes that could kill millions, yet would never see the immediate reality of death because the President remains thousands of miles away from the target.

Fisher proposed the following:
* Place the nuclear launch codes inside a small capsule.
* Implant this capsule right next to the heart of a volunteer.
* The volunteer would accompany the President at all times, carrying a large, heavy butcher knife.
* If the President ever decided to launch nuclear weapons, he would first have to kill the volunteer with his own hands to retrieve the codes. 

Fisher argued that the President must look at someone, realize what innocent death is, and see blood on the White House carpet before ordering millions of deaths. When Pentagon officials objected that this would distort the President's judgment, Fisher responded that it would actually restore it by making the cost of the decision immediate and tangible.

## Why it matters / connection
This example serves as a powerful metaphor for the inversion of the 4th Law of Behavior Change: **Make it unsatisfying/painful**. It demonstrates that the more local, tangible, and immediate a consequence is, the more likely it is to prevent destructive behavior. Conversely, global, vague, and delayed consequences (like the abstract idea of millions of deaths or long-term health decline) fail to influence decisions in the moment.
