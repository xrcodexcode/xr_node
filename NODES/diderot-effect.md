# The Diderot Effect

## Core idea
The Diderot Effect states that obtaining a new possession often creates a spiral of consumption that leads to additional, subsequent purchases. 

The concept is named after the French philosopher Denis Diderot, who lived in poverty until Empress Catherine the Great of Russia purchased his personal library for £1,000 (over $150,000 today). With his new wealth, Diderot acquired an elegant scarlet robe. However, he soon noticed that the beautiful robe looked out of place among his common possessions. To restore harmony, Diderot began upgrading his home: he replaced his rug with one from Damascus, bought expensive sculptures, added a mirror above the mantel, replaced his kitchen table, and traded his straw chair for a leather one. Each new acquisition triggered the next.

This pattern is a chain reaction visible in many areas of life (e.g., buying a dress requires new matching shoes and earrings; buying a couch prompts a living room layout redesign). In human behavior, no action occurs in isolation; the completion of one behavior naturally acts as the cue for the next.

## Why it matters / connection
The Diderot Effect explains the interconnected nature of human actions. While it can lead to negative spirals of consumer spending, it can also be harnessed positively. We can use the natural momentum of one behavior leading into another to chain productive habits together, which is the core mechanism behind habit stacking.
