# Economic Inefficiencies of Ethanol Blending

## Summary
India's E20 fuel blending policy exposes several structural economic inefficiencies and cost asymmetries. The domestic production cost of ethanol (~₹75/liter) remains significantly higher than the import and refining cost of pure petrol (~₹50/liter when global crude trades around $70/barrel). Despite using a more expensive domestic additive that decreases fuel efficiency, the government charges consumers the full petrol rate for the E20 blend, effectively imposing an hidden tax.

## Key Idea
The economics of ethanol blending in India function as a subsidy transfer to domestic sugar lobbies and agricultural mills, funded by vehicle owners who pay higher prices for fuel that provides less mileage and threatens engine durability.

## Details
* **Price Discrepancy:** Pure petrol cost at the refinery gate is low when crude oil prices are stable. However, domestic ethanol, manufactured from sugar sugarcane syrup or food grains, is price-supported by the state to guarantee farmers a baseline income, keeping it at ~₹75/liter.
* **Mileage and Calorific Loss:** Ethanol has a lower calorific value than petrol. A vehicle running on E20 fuel suffers a 10% to 15% reduction in mileage (average drop of 2-3 km/liter) compared to pure petrol.
* **Consumer Pricing Arbitrage:** Since pure petrol (E00) has been phased out at public gas pumps, consumers have no option to purchase unblended fuel. The government sells the E20 mixture at the same price as pure petrol, pocketing the price arbitrage and forcing consumers to pay more for lower-performing fuel (an instance of the "apathy tax").
* **Sugar Lobby Influence:** The fast-tracking of the E20 policy from 2030 to 2025 is largely attributed to the powerful political influence of sugar mill cooperatives. These mills secured highly profitable, long-term supply contracts for ethanol, guaranteeing their investments at the expense of consumer automotive health.
