# Moral Sacrifice in Social Protests

## Summary
In a David vs. Goliath power dynamic—where individual citizens (David) challenge a highly powerful state (Goliath)—protestors cannot rely solely on media campaigns or digital engagement to force concessions. To earn genuine public trust and social legitimacy, a movement requires demonstrable, high-stakes moral sacrifice (such as a prolonged hunger strike or physical risk). Without this sacrifice, the public and state easily dismiss the movement as a manufactured public relations stunt or a partisan campaign.

## Key Idea
Public trust in a protest is highly fragile; therefore, protestors must take personal, physical, or material damage upon themselves to prove the sincerity of their demands to the broader, non-affected populace.

## Details
* **David vs. Goliath Dynamic:** Because the state has a monopoly on power, violence, and communication platforms, the weaker citizen must appeal to the moral conscience of society.
* **The Sincerity Barrier:** In low-trust societies, the public assumes that political movements are funded by hidden interest groups (e.g. foreign funding, opposition parties). Physical sacrifice (such as Sonam Wangchuk's 21-day hunger strike) acts as an undeniable proof of sincerity because individuals are rarely willing to risk death or severe health damage for a fabricated cause.
* **Building a Public Symbol:** The moral weight of a hunger strike rises exponentially over time. While the public may ignore a strike in its initial days, by the 10th to 16th day, the gravity of the physical deterioration forces the public to recognize the urgency of the issue (e.g., Sonam Wangchuk's protest regarding exam paper leaks only captured national media attention after his fast crossed two weeks).
